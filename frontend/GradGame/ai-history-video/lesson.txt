Ancient Egypt: Civilization Along the Nile

Ancient Egypt was one of the longest-lasting civilizations in history. It flourished for thousands of years in northeastern Africa, centered on the Nile River. The Nile was Egypt’s lifeline: it gave fresh water, a highway for boats, and yearly floods that spread dark, fertile mud (“silt”) across the fields.

Farmers grew wheat and barley in that rich soil. A steady food supply supported towns, crafts, and trade. Writing helped officials keep records: Egyptian scribes used hieroglyphs—pictures and signs—on temple walls and papyrus scrolls.

Egypt was ruled by pharaohs, kings seen as linked to the gods. Famous rulers include Hatshepsut, a powerful female pharaoh, and Tutankhamun, whose intact tomb amazed the world when it was found in 1922.

The pyramids of Giza, especially the Great Pyramid built for Pharaoh Khufu, are among the ancient world’s most famous monuments. They were royal tombs, part of Egyptian beliefs about life after death. Careful mummification preserved the body; tombs were filled with goods for the journey beyond.

Egyptian engineers and artists left pyramids, temples, statues, and paintings that still teach us how they lived, believed, and organized one of history’s great societies.
